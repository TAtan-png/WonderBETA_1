History

The Darvaza crater, known as the "Gate of Hell", is one of the most fascinating places in Turkmenistan. It is a huge crater in the Karakum Desert that has been burning continuously for more than 50 years. Its origin is linked to an accident that occurred during the Soviet era.

Origin of the crater

In 1971, a team of Soviet geologists was exploring the area in search of natural gas. During drilling, the ground unexpectedly collapsed, creating a huge hole approximately 70 meters in diameter and 30 meters deep. The collapse was caused by the existence of an underground cavern filled with natural gas, causing the drilling equipment to fall inside.
To prevent the toxic gases from spreading through the air and affecting nearby towns, the scientists decided to set it on fire, believing that the gas would be consumed within a few days. However, the gas continues to burn to this day, more than 50 years later.

The crater today

Since then, the Darvaza crater has become a unique tourist attraction. Its constant fire, visible from miles away, has earned it the nickname "Hell's Gate". At night, the glow of the fire creates an impressive image in the middle of the desert.
In 2010, the then president of Turkmenistan, Gurbanguly Berdimuhamedow, ordered that the crater be studied and a way be found to extinguish it, because it wastes valuable natural gas resources. In 2022, his successor, Serdar Berdimuhamedow, announced plans to put it out permanently, although it continues to burn until now.

Geological and tourist importance

The crater is an example of the vast natural gas deposits of Turkmenistan, which has the fourth largest gas reserves in the world. Despite the government's attempts to close the crater, it remains a popular destination for tourists and adventurers looking to see this unique natural spectacle.
